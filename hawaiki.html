Hawaiki



Hawaiki is the name given in Māori tradition to the ancestral homeland from whence they came. It is a concept that is widespread throughout Polynesian culture in its various linguistic forms. Although Māori accounts of Hawaiki are steeped in the language of myth, they can be quite reliably understood as tribal migration origin stories. These vary from tribe to tribe, with some thought to describe internal migration within Aotearoa, whereas other traditions recount journeys from pacific islands further-afield. Although precise locations are rarely detailed, Ngāti Ruanui tradition is recorded as having the following whakataukī :

E kore au e ngaro; he kākano i ruia mat i Rangiātea.

I shall never be lost; the seed which was sown from Raiatea.1

This reference to Raꞌiatea as a Hawaiki for Māori tribal affiliation corresponds with various genetic, linguistic, botanical, and archaeological evidence which suggests the Society Islands as a likely starting point for various ancient Eastern Polynesian migrants.
