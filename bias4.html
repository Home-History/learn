Propaganda


Propaganda can be described as information, especially of a biased or misleading nature, used to influence or persuade an audience to further an agenda. It relies primarily on a manipulative approach to promoting political or idealogical viewpoints, and building the social consensus through groupthink.


Above are commonly recognized propaganda images from previous world wars. Propaganda is a term that is easily relatable to a tactic states use during war time to demonise the enemy, drive recruitment, or boost morale at home. However, less is understood generally about how propaganda is utilised in the media outside of periods of war, or there is a perception that propaganda is something that is only utilised elsewhere, in less understood foreign societies, by disreputable state leaders. In fact, propaganda is more omnipresent in our present day societies than many may realise.



Although the study and use of propaganda dates back centuries (or even millenia), in the 20th century propaganda was recognised for its potential to shape opinions are influence ideas in all facets of society. After its use by the German authorities to promote Nazi genocidal programmes, "propaganda" as a term became unsavoury to the public, yet its psychological techniques persisted, inspired by the prominent "father of public relations", American consultant to business and government Edward Bernays, author of books such as Crystallizing Public Opinion (1923), A Public Relations Counsel (1927) and Propaganda in 1928. As relevant to advertising as it is to politics, propaganda is just as commonplace today as at any other time in history, and its use even admitted to by politicians in recent times.

Research Task


The research task for this module on bias is really an ongoing task when doing research, or engaging in media of almost any description; that is, to approach the source crtically and be on the lookout for any signs that it may be biased, as we have done in the exercise on the previous page. Sometimes this is easy, and at other times unrecognisable.

It may seem unreasonable to be searching all sources and media for potential bias all the time, and it is. Although ideally we would be able to pick up on any bias present, without personal expertise in every subject and unlimited time in the day this is often just not possible. Sometimes we will rely on others for information based on our confidence of their reputation or character (for example, authors of secondary sources). In this, we should trust in our intuition as well as our critical senses, and if in doubt, dig deeper into what is being said and by who.
