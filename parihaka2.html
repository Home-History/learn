Parihaka cont.


In the years that followed we can see various ways that the "invasion", "trouble", or "difficulty" at Parihaka, and its aftermath was recounted by authors. For many decades the incident was largely sidelined and only spoken of in passing. However, in some books we can find various published recollections of what happened at Parihaka on the 5th of November and what it would come to mean for the the region and nation.

Writing in a local history of Hāwera in 1904, William Alphonsus Quin tells of the threat that Parihaka was perceived to be, where:

it was known that there were fully sixteen hundred able-bodied Maoris in the prophet’s pa at the time, there appeared to be every prospect of the vexed Maori question being finally decided in a pitched battle.1

Luckily, to the authors mind, this crisis was evaded thanks to the bold action of the "gallant" Major Tuke, who:

stepping over the forms of the muttering young men... quietly arrested the prophet, and sent him out with an escort. The bloodless conquest of Parihaka was thus satisfactorily accomplished, and the turbulent prophets sent to the South Island to cool their fevered imaginations.2

In the book Fifty Years of Volunteering published in 1910, the writer Lieut.-Colonel H. Slater, V.D. concurs with Quin in the praising of:

the force that marched on Parihaka in November, 1881, and who, though no shot was fired, destroyed the mana of Te Whiti, and practically settled the Native scare for ever.3

Further to the "congratulation that the Parihaka campaign was a bloodless one", Slater included a quote from the Lyttelton Times stating that:

“The Native difficulty has given us many bad legacies. But it has given us one good thing, it has given us evidence of the sterling character and patriotic spirit of the Volunteer Force."4

William Pember Reeves, in his seminal historical book The Long White Cloud in 1898 was "one of a small minority of New Zealanders who thought that our (Pākehā) neglect to make the (land) reserves put us in the wrong in the affair". He summarised the affair thus:

It is true that the delays in redeeming promises concerning reserves to be made and given back from the confiscated Maori territory were allowed to remain a grievance for more than another decade, and led, as late as 1880, to interference by the natives with road-making in some of this lost land of theirs in Taranaki. There, round a prophet named Te Whiti, flocked numbers of natives sore with a sense of injustice. Though Te Whiti was as pacific as eccentric, the Government, swayed by the alarm and irritation thus aroused, took the extreme step of pouring into his village of Parihaka an overwhelming armed force. Then, after reading the Riot Act to a passive and orderly crowd of men, women, and children, they proceeded to make wholesale arrests, to evict the villagers, and to destroy houses and crops. Public opinion, which had conjured up the phantom of an imminent native rising, supported the proceeding. There was no such danger, for the natives were not supplied with arms...5

However, Reeves concluded his account, opining that:

the breaking up of Parihaka was at last followed up by a reasonable and liberal settlement of the long-delayed Reserves question, a settlement in which Sir William Fox and Sir F. Dillon Bell did humane and honourable service, it may be classed as the last of the long series of native alarms. There will be no more Maori wars.6


Later Interpretations


In later decades, authors became demonstrably more sympathetic to the treatment of Māori during the 1800's. First published in 1959, Keith Sinclair, in his book A History of New Zealand, described the situation at Parihaka leading up to 1881 as follows:

(Te Whiti's) followers lived a virtuous and industrious life, but they were settled on land which had been confiscated. The Maoris believed, no doubt rightly, that McLean (who died in 1878) had promised them reserves; but the Government began to survey the land before these were granted. Te Whiti launched a passive resistance campaign. Parties of Maoris pulled out survey pegs, built fences across the disputed land, and began ploughing up neighbouring European farms as a form of protest. These tactics infuriated the settlers to the point of hysteria.7

A cultural shift was certainly in motion by the latter half of the 20th century. Pākehā author Dick Scott in 1975 published Ask That Mountain: The Story of Parihaka, to address what he termed a "hitherto overwhelmingly one-sided record, it was an impatient and angry attempt, but a beginning".8 However, to some not angry enough, as a reviewer for the Victoria University's student magazine Salient wrote:

...ASK ANY MOUNTAIN [sic] is a profoundly disturbing documentary of an episode of our history that requires much greater reappraisal than occurs at present, but is handicapped in several respects. There is not sufficient anger over what happened, and the wishy-washy 'answers' provided are no real solution.
So long as New Zealand society is infected by the rampant individualism of capitalist attitudes communal ideals such as those of Parihaka can find little place, and true respect for a communal culture is impossible.9

Ask That Mountain was followed by Tony Simpson's Te Riri Pakeha: The White Man's Anger in 1979, in which he determined to show the intended (specifically Pākehā) readership, that "the treasured belief that New Zealand is the most succesful multiracial society in the world is a delusion".10

This re-telling of the wars of the 19th c. (previously referred to commonly as "The Maori Wars"), with a focus on Māori perspectives, continued throughout the following decades with books like James Belich's The New Zealand Wars and the Victorian Interpretation of Racial Conflict (1986), right through to recent years with Vincent O'Malley's The New Zealand Wars | Ngā Pakanga o Aotearoa in 2019.
