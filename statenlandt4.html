Staten Landt cont.


That same day following the bloody altercation, Tasman and the crew of the Heemskerck and Zeehaen resolved to leave behind “this place of murderers” in search of a more convenient spot to obtain water and refreshments. This encounter had taught them “to hold the inhabitants of this land as enemies”, bestowing upon the place of the incident the name Murderers’ (Moordenaar's) Bay, today known (somewhat ironically) as Golden Bay. Continuing north, they charted a course up the west coast of the North Island as far as Cape Maria van Diemen and the Three Kings Islands, the two remaining geographic features in the country to still today retain the names Tasman had given them. Of the land itself, Tasman determined that it seemed to be “a very fine country, and we trust that it is part of the great coast of the unknown Zuytlandt (South Land). Believing it possible that what they had happened upon was connected to the same land that his countrymen Schouten and LeMaire had previously found while looking for a new route to the Moluccas, located off the coast of South America*, Tasman bestowed this upon the map of this land its first given name; Staete Landt. Later found to be in error, it was renamed Zeelandia Nova, or New Zealand




* It was discovered in 1643 by the Dutch Admiral Hendrick Brouwer 1643 that Staten Land was in fact an island, and therefore could not possibly extend to the region of Tasman’s discovery. Today, this land is known as Isla de los Estados, or Staten Island, in Argentina.
