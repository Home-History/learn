Parihaka cont.


When searching the internet for Parihaka, one of the first results you will find is Invasion of pacifist settlement at Parihaka, from the website NZ History (Ministry for Culture and Heritage). Here you can find a succinct summary of how the events that unfolded around Parihaka in the latter half of the 19th c. are understood today. Below is an abridged quote taken from the article to illustrate a basic modern understanding of the events of the 5th of November, 1881:

Founded in the mid-1860s, Parihaka was soon attracting dispossessed and disillusioned Māori from around the country. They were impressed by the kaupapa of its main leaders, Te Whiti-o-Rongomai and Tohu Kākahi, both of the Taranaki and Te Āti Awa iwi.
When in May 1879 the colonial government moved to occupy fertile land on the Waimate Plains that had been declared confiscated in the 1860s, Te Whiti and Tohu developed tactics of non-violent resistance.
On 5 November, about 1600 volunteers and Constabulary Field Force troops marched on Parihaka. Several thousand Māori sat quietly on the marae as singing children greeted the force led by Native Minister John Bryce. The Whanganui farmer had fought in the campaign against Tītokowaru (see 9 June) and viewed Parihaka as a ‘headquarters of fanaticism and disaffection’. Bryce ordered the arrest of Parihaka’s leaders, the destruction of much of the village and the dispersal of most of its inhabitants. The Sim Commission which investigated these events in the 1920s was told that women were raped by troops, with some bearing children as a result.1


Contemporary Reports


But how was the "invasion of (the) pacifist settlement at Parihaka" reported in the day? Leading up to the 5th of November, newspaper headlines variously described the unrest at Parihaka as...


Behind such scurrilous headlines ran stories that sought to disgust readers as to the state of the Parihaka settlement. A narrative was being formulated of a people residing in degradation and heretical fanaticism. The article to the left, "Dirty Parihaka", published in the West Coast Times, 14 September 1881, is a particuarly blunt example of such reporting.

Other newspaper reports reinforced the notion of those at Parihaka as squatters, residing illegally on land that they had no rights to. The journalists noted that it was "hoped the Constabulary will take some action in the matter".2

In the days leading up to the "action" taken by government troops, the press reported favourably on the "fine, smart set of young fellows" that made up the Taranaki Mounted Rifles, who were to join with the more than 1400 men and march on Parihaka.3 Evidently, they were looking forward to being allowed to witness the novel events about to unfold, despite the "stoppage of all public traffic... through the Parihaka block", where "the only persons who will be permitted to be present at the entrance to Parihaka, in addition to the "force," are Ministers and their staffs, and... Press correspondents".3

However, members of the press were to be disappointed, and the "action" they were hoping for instead became "A Wretched Fiasco", as journalists vented their frustation at being barred from the events at Parihaka, as was reported by the Christchurch Star:

Dismay and consternation spread through the ranks of newspaper correspondents at Pungarehu on Friday when it became known late in the day that all civilians were forbidden to follow the advance of the forces on Parihaka, and would be arrested if found there on the entrance of the troops. Several journals had gone to great expense in sending men to the front ; and the wrath of these may be more easily imagined than put into polite language. The occasion was one of such extreme interest to people, whatever their opinions in the matter, that specials had been despatched by some papers for the express purpose of being on the spot during the one eventful day alone. Every effort was made to obtain a relaxation of the Draconian law, but in vain.4
