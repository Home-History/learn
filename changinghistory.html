5. Changing History


Now we will turn to a study within the field of history that, well, describes the study of history. Things are about to get a bit meta...

Historiography


Earlier in the course we broadly defined history as something like a study of past events. So how does the past change? While the events that happened and the people that lived in the past don't change, how they are talked or written about often does. Historiography refers to the entire body of work about a given historical subject. When studying the historiography of a historical subject, you may find that interpretations of what happened in the past change throughout time. This is all the more likely for topics considered controversial. This is sometimes referred to as historical revisionism.


There are many reasons that history may be revised over time. These include:



  • Access to new evidence: If any previously unknown sources or evidence are discovered it may change how past events are interpreted.


  • New scientific developments: Like new evidence, access to new scientific developments may contribute to reinterpretation of historical events (eg. carbon dating of historical sites).


  • Political or cultural change: When there is a shift in the political or cultural social climate, previously ignored or suppressed evidence may come to light, or certain historical interpretations may be emphasized over others.
